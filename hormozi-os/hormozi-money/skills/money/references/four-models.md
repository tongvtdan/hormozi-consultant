# Four Money Models — Reference
**Source: $100M Money Models — Alex Hormozi / ACQ Framework**

> "There are only four ways to make money. You either sell products, services, subscriptions, or assets. Pick the right one for your stage."

---

## Model 1 — Products

**Core definition:** A one-time exchange of money for a discrete deliverable.

### Physical Products

**Economics:**
- Revenue = Units × Price
- Gross Profit = Revenue − COGS (Cost of Goods Sold)
- Typical gross margins: 30–70% (higher for premium; lower for commodity)

**Scaling levers:**
- Average Order Value (AOV) — bundle, upsell, premium tiers
- Units sold — distribution, advertising, retail partnerships
- Margin — manufacturing efficiency, supplier negotiation, premium positioning

**Key risks:**
- Inventory management (holding cost, stockouts)
- Returns and logistics complexity
- Commoditization if not differentiated

---

### Digital Products

**Economics:**
- Revenue = Units × Price
- Gross Profit ≈ Revenue (near-zero marginal cost after creation)
- Typical gross margins: 85–95%

**Scaling levers:**
- Hook quality (drives discovery and conversion)
- SEO and content (organic distribution)
- Proof (reviews, testimonials, case studies)

**Key risks:**
- Piracy and unauthorized sharing
- Market saturation (easy to copy)
- Trust deficit (anyone can claim expertise)

## Model 2 — Subscriptions

**Core definition:** Recurring payment for ongoing access, delivered value, or continued service.

### Why Subscriptions Win at Scale

| Metric | Product Business | Subscription Business |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue predictability | Low | Very high (MRR visibility) |
| Valuation multiple | 1–3× revenue | 5–15× ARR |
| CAC recovery | 1 transaction | Amortized over lifespan |
| Growth compounding | Linear | Exponential (if churn < growth) |

### The Churn Math (most critical subscription metric)

```
Net Revenue Retention (NRR) = (MRR at end of period − Churn MRR − Contraction MRR + Expansion MRR) ÷ MRR at start

Target: > 100% NRR (means you grow revenue even with no new customers)
```

**Churn rate benchmarks:**
- World-class SaaS: < 2%/month (< 24%/year)
- Healthy: 3–5%/month
- Danger zone: > 8%/month

### Subscription Offer Types

| Type | Example | Churn Driver | Retention Fix |
|---|---|---|---|
| SaaS (tool) | CRM, design tool | Product stops working for them | Activation + AHA moment in week 1 |
| Membership (community) | Mastermind, Discord | Value not exceeding cost | AMAs, exclusives, community wins |
| Content subscription | Newsletter, courses | Content goes stale | Freshness, curation, community |
| Service retainer | Agency, consulting | Results plateau | New initiatives, proactive value delivery |
| Physical subscription | Monthly box | Novelty wears off | Curation quality, personalization |

## Model 3 — Services

**Core definition:** Payment for human labor, expertise, or outcomes.

### The DFY/DWY/DIY Spectrum

| Model | Description | Price Range | Capacity | Margin |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| DFY — Done For You | You do the work for them | $2,000–$100,000+ | Limited by team size | 40–70% |
| DWY — Done With You | You guide, they execute | $1,000–$30,000 | Moderate (group scaling) | 60–80% |
| DIY — Do It Yourself | They execute, you provide tools | $200–$5,000 | Unlimited (product-like) | 80–95% |

**Service to subscription transition:**
The natural evolution is: DFY service → DWY program → DIY tool/course → subscription

Each step increases scalability and margin while reducing customization.

### The Service Pricing Paradox

> "Service businesses undercharge because they price their labor. They should price the outcome."

**Wrong:** $150/hour × 20 hours = $3,000
**Right:** "We generate 15 qualified leads/month for you" = $3,000/month

Outcome pricing removes the time/value trap. It creates unlimited upside for efficiency.

**How to convert to outcome pricing:**
1. Define the measurable result you deliver
2. Calculate what that result is worth to the client
3. Price at 10–30% of the value delivered
4. Guarantee the result to reduce risk perception

## Model 4 — Assets

**Core definition:** Owning something that generates cash flow with minimal ongoing labor.

### Asset Types for Operators

| Asset Type | Capital Required | Expected Yield | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Business equity (minority) | High | 15–40%+ IRR | Best for operators with capital + deal flow |
| White-label licensing | Low | Royalty stream | Requires proven, licensable system |
| Digital assets (SEO sites, newsletters) | Low-Medium | 20–40% of revenue | Requires content moat |
| SaaS equity | Medium | 5–15× ARR at exit | Best path when software can replace service |
| Real estate (commercial) | High | 6–12% cap rate | Inflation hedge; less liquid |
| Franchise model | Medium | Fee + royalty stream | Requires documented operating system |

**Hormozi's Asset Strategy:**
The acquisition.com model: invest in service businesses with great operators but weak acquisition. Provide the acquisition system (hormozi-offers + hormozi-leads) in exchange for equity.

**For most founder-operators:**
Start with services. Build subscriptions. Productize into digital assets. Then deploy capital into equity.

## Model Combination Strategy (by revenue stage)

| Stage | Primary Model | Add Next |
|---|---|---|
| $0–$10k/month | Services (fastest cash) | — |
| $10k–$100k/month | Services | + Digital product (leverage) |
| $100k–$500k/month | Services | + Subscription/retainer (predictability) |
| $500k–$2M/month | Services + Subscription | + Licensing or digital asset |
| $2M+/month | All 3 above | + Equity/asset deployment |

**Rule:** Never add a new model before the existing one is optimized. See More/Better/New (Workflow 4).
